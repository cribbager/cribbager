How to Play Cribbage

Cribbage is a card game for two players (the version here) built around a race to 121 points. This guide covers the rules only — enough to sit down and play a full hand. It assumes no prior knowledge; every term is defined the first time it appears.

Quick rules

  • Players & goal: two players race to be first to 121 points.
  • The deal: each player is dealt 6 cards; the deal alternates each hand.
  • The crib: each player throws 2 cards into a separate 4-card hand called the crib, which belongs to the dealer.
  • The starter: the non-dealer cuts the deck and the dealer turns up one card, the starter, used by every hand when counting.
  • The play (pegging): players alternately lay cards and call the running total, never going past 31, scoring for fifteens, thirty-ones, pairs, runs, and the last card.
  • The show: after the play, each player counts their hand plus the starter for fifteens, pairs, runs, flushes, and his nobs. The non-dealer counts first.
  • The crib: the dealer then counts the crib the same way, as a bonus hand.
  • Winning: the first player to reach 121 wins — the game can end mid-count.

1. The object & the board

The object of cribbage is to be the first player to score 121 points. Points are earned in two ways in each hand: during the play and during the count (also called the show).

Scores are tracked on a cribbage board, a peg board with two rows of 60 holes plus a final game hole per player. You move a peg forward one hole per point, going up the board and back down — "twice around" a standard board is 120, and the last hole is 121. Because you always have two pegs, the rearmost peg shows your previous score and the front peg your current one, so it is easy to see how many points each play was worth.

A game is a series of hands. Each hand follows the same sequence: deal, discard to the crib, cut the starter, the play, then the count. The deal passes to the other player after each hand, and hands repeat until someone reaches 121.

2. The deal

Using a standard 52-card deck, the dealer deals 6 cards to each player, one at a time, face down. (In two-player cribbage it is 6 cards each; other player counts use 5.) Players pick up and look at their own six cards.

The role of dealer alternates every hand. Being dealer matters because the dealer owns the crib (see below), so over a game the small advantage of the crib is shared evenly. To decide who deals first, players usually each cut the deck and the lower card deals.

3. The crib (discard)

Before the play begins, each player chooses 2 of their 6 cards to discard face down into the crib — a separate, fifth hand of four cards. This leaves each player with the four cards they will keep and play.

The crib belongs to the dealer: at the end of the hand the dealer counts it as an extra hand for points. So when you discard, you are helping your own crib if you are the dealer, and helping your opponent's crib if you are not. The non-dealer is sometimes called the pone.

4. The cut & the starter

After the crib is set, the non-dealer cuts the deck (lifts off a portion), and the dealer turns up the top card of the bottom portion. This face-up card is the starter (also called the "cut" or "turn-up").

The starter is not played. Instead it acts as a shared fifth card for every hand and the crib when counting at the end of the hand.

If the starter is a Jack, the dealer immediately pegs 2 points. This is called "his heels" (or "his nibs"). It is the only score that comes straight from the cut.

5. The play (pegging)

Now the players play their four cards in alternating turns, starting with the non-dealer. As each card is laid down, the player says the new running total of all cards played so far. Face cards (J, Q, K) count as 10, aces count as 1, and every other card counts its face value.

The running total may never exceed 31. On your turn you must play a card if you can do so without going over 31. Scoring during the play is called pegging, because points are pegged the moment they happen:

Pegging scores (during the play)
You score…When…Points
FifteenYour card brings the running total to exactly 152
Thirty-oneYour card brings the running total to exactly 312
PairYou match the rank of the card just played2
Pair royal (three of a kind)A third card of that rank in a row6

When a player cannot play without going over 31, they say "go". The other player then pegs 1 for the go and continues playing any cards they still can. When neither player can play, the player who laid the last card pegs 1 for the last card (or 2 if it hit exactly 31). The running total then resets to zero and play continues with any remaining cards, starting with the player after the one who last played. Pairs and runs only count within the current run of cards — they are broken by a reset.

6. Counting the show

After all cards are played, players pick their four cards back up and count them for points. This is the show. Each player counts their four hand cards plus the starter as a five-card group. The non-dealer counts first — this matters near the end of a game, because reaching 121 first wins even if the dealer would have counted more.

Counting scores (the show)
You score…For…Points
FifteenEach distinct combination of cards adding to exactly 152
PairEach pair of cards of the same rank2
RunEach run of 3+ consecutive ranks1 per card
Flush (hand)All four hand cards the same suit4
Flush (with starter)All four hand cards and the starter the same suit5
His nobsHolding the Jack of the same suit as the starter1

A few clarifications that trip up beginners:

Fifteens count every distinct combination separately. For example, if three different cards each total 15 with others, each combination scores 2. Pairs from three of a kind are worth 6 (three pairs), and four of a kind is worth 12. Runs score one point per card, and if you hold a duplicate rank a "double run" scores each run separately (e.g. 4-5-5-6 is two runs of three plus a pair). Face cards all count 10 for fifteens but keep their own rank for pairs and runs (a Jack and Queen are not a pair, but they are consecutive for a run).

7. The crib count

After both players have counted their hands, the dealer turns over the crib and counts it too — again as four cards plus the starter, using exactly the same scoring rules. The crib is a bonus that only the dealer scores, which is why the deal alternates.

There is one difference: a crib flush needs all five cards of the same suit (the four crib cards and the starter). A four-card flush counts in a hand but never in the crib.

8. Winning

The first player to reach 121 points wins immediately. Because scoring is incremental — pegged during the play and tallied combination-by-combination during the count — the game can end mid-count. As soon as a peg reaches the final hole the game is over, even if the player had more points left to count. This is why the non-dealer counting first can be decisive in a close game.

Glossary

The crib
The fifth hand, made of the two cards each player discards. It is counted by the dealer at the end of the hand.
Pone
The non-dealer. The pone plays first during the play and counts their hand first during the show.
The cut / the starter
The face-up card turned after the non-dealer cuts the deck. It is shared by every hand and the crib when counting; it is not played during the play.
His heels
2 points the dealer pegs when the starter card is a Jack. Sometimes called "his nibs."
His nobs
1 point for holding the Jack of the same suit as the starter, scored during the count. (Different from "his heels," which comes from the cut.)
Go
What you say when you cannot play a card without the running total exceeding 31. The other player pegs 1 for the go.
Pegging
Scoring points during the play, so called because the points are moved on the board immediately.
Muggins (optional)
An optional house rule: if you overlook points in your own count, an alert opponent may claim them by calling "muggins." Cribbager does not use muggins — your hand is always scored in full.
